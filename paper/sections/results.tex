\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The results answer the production question directly: with the candidate universe, target surface, and
record budget fixed, which retained weight vector best fits the current \populace{} target surface? All
methods are scored on the full set of 31{,}534 materialized targets, using the penalty-free Populace
objective loss in Equation~\ref{eq:loss_cal}. The run uses three seeds at each requested record budget.
Raw absolute-relative-error (ARE) summaries, effective sample size, and max weight are reported as
diagnostics around that loss.

\subsection{The full-surface accuracy frontier}

Figure~\ref{fig:objective_frontier} is the headline result. The target-informed $L_0$ subset is not the
most accurate method on the current full surface. Random sampling followed by gradient reweighting has the
lowest Populace objective loss at every budget except the smallest, where survey-weight sampling is
slightly lower. At the 10{,}000-record anchor, random + reweight reaches a 46.3\% objective loss, survey-
weight sampling 50.8\%, informed $L_0$ plus refit 55.4\%, and the raw informed $L_0$ gated weights
72.2\%. At 40{,}000 records the ranking narrows but does not reverse: random + reweight is 44.1\%,
informed $L_0$ plus refit 46.4\%, survey-weight sampling 47.7\%, and informed $L_0$ 69.0\%.

The post-selection refit is therefore essential. Refitting ordinary calibration weights on the selected
$L_0$ records lowers the objective by 11--23 percentage points relative to the raw gated weights and
brings the selected subset close to the sampling baselines at larger budgets. But the refit does not turn
the current $L_0$ selector into the best full-surface sampler. The current implementation is better read
as a working target-informed selection mechanism whose weights need refitting, not as an accuracy win over
random + reweight on this target surface.

Table~\ref{tab:sampling_comparison} reports the same comparison at the representative
$\sim$10{,}000-record budget, with raw ARE and weight diagnostics. The raw median ARE follows the
objective ordering: random + reweight has the lowest median error (68.8\%), followed by survey-weight
sampling (71.2\%), informed $L_0$ plus refit (83.0\%), and raw informed $L_0$ (95.5\%). The raw mean ARE
is much larger because a tail of denominator-degenerate targets has tiny values relative to a single
record's contribution (Section~\ref{sec:results-degenerate}).

The paired comparison in Table~\ref{tab:paired_comparison} makes the gap explicit. Against random +
reweight, informed $L_0$ plus refit has a higher median ARE at every budget above the smallest; the gap is
14.3 percentage points at 10{,}000 records and 19.2 points at 20{,}000 records. On the tail-sensitive
mean, the gap is not stable enough to claim a win either way. At 40{,}000 records the mean AREs are nearly
level, but the median still favours random + reweight. With three seeds, these paired intervals should be
read as descriptive uncertainty rather than formal inference.

\subsection{Size and concentration}
\label{sec:results-controls}

The size control works even though the accuracy result is negative. The eight-step bisection over
$\lambda_{L_0}$ hits the requested budgets closely: the achieved retained counts average 2{,}079, 5{,}034,
9{,}770, 19{,}319, and 41{,}004 records for the five requested budgets (Appendix~\ref{app:presets}). This
is a real operational advantage over manually choosing a cutoff on fitted weights.

The weight distribution is the cost. Figure~\ref{fig:usability} shows that informed $L_0$ and the
post-$L_0$ refit concentrate far more mass than the sampling baselines. At the 10{,}000-record anchor, the
effective sample size is 393 for informed $L_0$ plus refit, 219 for raw informed $L_0$, 1{,}624 for random
+ reweight, and 4{,}129 for survey-weight sampling. The largest weight in the post-$L_0$ refit is about
6.4 million, compared with 1.2 million for random + reweight and 0.55 million for survey-weight sampling.
The target-informed selector is finding records that matter for the targets, but the resulting subset is
still too reliant on a small number of high-weight records.

\subsection{Accuracy by geography}

Table~\ref{tab:calibration_accuracy} breaks the informed $L_0$ plus refit diagnostics down by geography at
the anchor budget. District targets are the hardest part of the current surface: their median ARE is
91.3\%, compared with 39.4\% for state targets and 37.6\% for national targets. This is the intended stress
test for the method, because the congressional-district surface is exactly why \populace{} needs to
construct a large candidate universe before pruning it. It is also where the current selector is not yet
delivering enough fit.

\subsection{Degenerate targets and metric robustness}
\label{sec:results-degenerate}

The full surface contains 8{,}095 denominator-degenerate targets: targets whose value is smaller than the
largest single-record contribution at the initial weight. Almost all of the largest raw AREs are IRS SOI
cells in this class. These targets are real source-backed facts and are retained in the headline objective,
but the raw ARE mean is not a reliable summary of typical fit because a small absolute miss can imply an
enormous relative error.

For transparency, the report names the degenerate targets in \texttt{degenerate\_targets.csv} and
recomputes the mean after dropping exactly those named rows. For informed $L_0$ plus refit, that targeted
removal lowers the mean ARE from 2{,}383.5\% to 151.1\% at the 10{,}000-record budget, and from
1{,}540.5\% to 84.6\% at 40{,}000 records. The median is much less sensitive to this tail, which is why the
paper reports it beside the Populace objective rather than leading with the raw mean.

\subsection{Cost}

Appendix~\ref{app:cost} reports runtime against the headline split's median ARE. Informed $L_0$ is
expensive because each budget requires an outer bisection over $\lambda_{L_0}$, while the two sampling
baselines reuse a dense fit or refit a sampled subset. The current full-surface results therefore do not
justify $L_0$ on accuracy alone. The research value is narrower but useful: the code now runs the full
surface, preserves every weight vector, imports Populace's own scoring/loss helpers, and shows where the
target-informed selector must improve before it can beat simpler baselines.
