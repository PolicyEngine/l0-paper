\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

The calibration problem is defined by two inputs: the household microdata that form the candidate
universe, and the administrative targets the calibrated dataset must reproduce. This section
describes how \microplex{} assembles the candidate microdata and how this study selects its targets.
The pipeline is the harness that produces the calibration problem; it is not itself the object of
evaluation.

\subsection{Microplex as a construction engine}

\microplex{} builds a microsimulation dataset from a declarative specification. The specification
names the data sources, the variables each source contributes, the rule for combining sources into
one record universe, the imputation graph that fills missing variables, the geographic assignment,
the calibration targets, and the calibration method. \microplex{} provides the engine and all
execution logic: it reads that specification and runs the stages in order, while the country-specific
content is supplied as a country pack that becomes the input to the engine's functions rather than
living in the engine itself. The engine validates the specification before running and pins external
inputs by content hash, so a build is reproducible.

Because each stage is declared rather than hard-coded, the imputation model and the calibration
method are interchangeable: a different imputation model, or a different calibration routine, can be
substituted without rewriting the pipeline, and the method studied here is one calibration option
among several the engine supports. The same declarative structure makes the steps that enlarge the
candidate universe explicit, namely combining sources, imputing onto more records, and creating
geographic variants, so the size of the candidate dataset is a property of the configuration rather
than an accident of the code.

\subsection{Base microdata}

\subsubsection{Current Population Survey}

The primary source is the Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement
\citep[CPS ASEC;][]{census2024}, a nationally representative household survey from the US Census
Bureau and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. The CPS provides demographic structure, household
relationships, labour income, transfer income, and reported participation in programmes such as
SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, and TANF.

The CPS is not a complete tax-benefit dataset on its own. Some tax variables are missing, others are
measured with error, and top incomes are imperfectly captured relative to tax records
\citep{burkhauser2012}. Benefit receipt is also underreported relative to administrative sources
\citep{rothbaum2021, meyer2021}. The pipeline therefore augments the CPS before calibration.

\subsubsection{Imputation from administrative and survey sources}

The pipeline imputes tax variables from the IRS Public Use File (PUF) onto CPS records following
\citet{woodruff2024}. The PUF carries detailed tax-return information but lacks household structure
and demographic coverage. Quantile regression forests \citep{meinshausen2006quantile} predict the
tax variables on the CPS from the PUF, imputing variables in sequence so that later variables
condition on earlier imputed values. The pipeline draws additional variables from other surveys
where the CPS is thin, including rent and real-estate tax, asset and net-worth components, and
selected income items. \tbc[exact source and variable list for this build]

Each imputation step adds information, and several add variants of the same household. Combined with
geographic assignment, these steps expand the candidate universe well beyond the size of the
original survey.

\subsubsection{Geographic variation}

To support subnational targets, the pipeline assigns records to geographies below the level the
survey identifies. A record can appear in more than one candidate placement, which raises the
candidate count and gives the calibration step the freedom to match local totals.

\subsection{Calibration targets}

The target system combines aggregates from several administrative sources at the national, state,
and district levels. This study does not calibrate to every target the pipeline could construct. It
calibrates to a prioritized subset, and it reports that subset explicitly rather than describing it
as a complete or maximal target set. Appendix~\ref{app:targets} lists the target families used and
the reason for including them. The exact counts come from each experiment's run manifest rather than
from a pipeline-wide inventory.
