\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the sampling comparison establishes}

The full-surface comparison rejects the strongest version of the $L_0$ hypothesis for the current
implementation. If the claim is that target-informed gates should beat a random subset at the same record
budget on the actual Populace objective, the evidence does not support it. Random sampling followed by
gradient reweighting is the best-performing method on the production loss over most of the frontier, and
survey-weight sampling is competitive throughout. Informed $L_0$ plus refit improves substantially on the
raw gated weights, but the selected subset is not better enough to overcome the simpler baseline.

That negative result is useful. First, it shows that the experiment is now measuring the right object: the
full materialized target surface, scored with the Populace loss, using the final retained weight vector
rather than a solver-side proxy. Second, it separates selection from weighting. Raw informed $L_0$ performs
poorly because the gated weights returned by the joint optimization are not good enough; the post-selection
refit recovers a large share of that gap, which means the selected records contain signal but need ordinary
calibration after selection. Third, it points to the next methodological question: whether the selector can
be improved without increasing weight concentration.

\subsection{What the controls establish}

The operational control over record count works. The outer bisection over $\lambda_{L_0}$ hits the target
size closely across the five requested budgets, and it does so without an arbitrary post-hoc cutoff. That
is still a useful property for a build-large-then-prune pipeline: the output size is an optimization target
rather than an accident of a threshold.

The weight-concentration diagnostics are less favorable. The informed subsets have much lower effective
sample size and much larger maximum weights than the sampling baselines. This is not just an aesthetic
problem: a dataset can match calibration aggregates while becoming fragile for downstream estimands that
depend on the distribution of weights. In the current run, any future $L_0$ variant has to improve not only
the Populace loss but also the ESS/max-weight frontier.

\subsection{Trade-offs and limitations}

\subsubsection{Target surface}

The target set is the current materialized Populace surface, not a hand-pruned paper subset. It is still
conditional on what the source facts and current PolicyEngine variables can materialize, and the empirical
claims are tied to the exact targets in each run's manifest. A different Ledger surface, PolicyEngine
version, materialization registry, or production weighting scheme could change the comparison.

\subsubsection{Random baselines}

The random + reweight baseline is strong because it uses the same gradient calibrator as the $L_0$ method
after drawing the subset. It is therefore not a straw baseline: it is the practical reduce-first,
calibrate-after workflow an analyst could run today. The result also clarifies the role of random seeds.
The paper averages over three seeds to measure run-to-run variation, but the workflow should not assume an
analyst will repeatedly resample and cherry-pick the best random draw.

\subsubsection{Degenerate targets and raw error metrics}

The raw mean ARE is dominated by denominator-degenerate targets, mostly IRS SOI cells where one record can
move a tiny target by more than the target's own value. The paper therefore leads with the Populace
objective loss and reports raw median/mean ARE as supplemental diagnostics. We do not hide the degenerate
targets: they are named in the audit, and the targeted-removal sensitivity shows how much they contribute
to the raw mean.

\subsubsection{Computational cost}

Informed $L_0$ is more expensive than the baselines because every budget requires an outer bisection over
the sparsity penalty. That cost is only worth paying if the selected subset is materially better, or if the
method delivers an operational constraint that the baselines cannot. On the current full-surface results,
the accuracy case is not yet there.

\subsection{Generalizability}

The data engineering here is specific to the United States, but the question is general. Many
microsimulation projects combine sources into a candidate dataset larger than they can ship, and then have
to reduce it. This run shows that target-informed sampling can be evaluated on the real production loss and
target surface, but it also shows that a simple random subset plus a strong calibrator is hard to beat. The
next round should treat random + reweight as the benchmark to surpass, not as a weak baseline.
